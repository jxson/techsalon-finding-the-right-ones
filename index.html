Terminology

engineers != developers

Developer

Developers or "Software Developers" are what you need.

A software developer is a person concerned with facets of the software development process. Their work includes researching, designing, implementing, and testing software.

All shapes and sizes

  • front-end
  • back-end
  • dev-ops

For most of the infrastructure you are building there are a few types of developers or specializations:

  • front-end: usually this means interface developer for browser, usually involves JavaScript, HTML, and CSS. Some are well versed in design.
  • back-end: (server side) basically, they don't mess with browser code. Usually this kind of developer works with http interfaces, application code and databases.
  • dev-ops: (systems administration - NOT I.T.) specializes in keeping servers running. Cloud infrastructure, infrastructure management.

Developers are your workers, they are the people who will build it for you.

Your Product

  • Why does your product exist?
  • What is your product?

Why?

What problem are you going to solve? Why does your product exist (or why are you trying to bring it to existence)?

We all want to feel like we are working on something worthwhile.

What?

What exactly is you product? Specifically how does it address it's existence (why). This should be:

  • simple
  • specific
  • special

Have a succinct product vision before talking to potential hires.

  • Instill confidence that you have a clear and understandable vison.
  • Ensure you're hiring people with a clear understanding of your product.

Have a succinct product vision before even talking to potential hires. Having at least these two questions thought out well before hand will be really helpful with:

  • Instilling confidence in your potential hires that you have a clear and understandable vision.
  • Make sure that your hiring developers with a very clear understanding of your vision.

When hiring you are looking for partners in helping you address the why of your product. Being able to quickly and succinctly explain this to potential developers will help you make sure you are establishing relationships with people who care about the problems they are solving and that their goals are aligned with yours.

You are competing with google, you will need to appeal to your developers in different ways.

Creating Relationships

Converse about your problem set and challenges.

Most developers nauturally want to help you find solutions.

Some keys to a good relationship are:

  • consistency
  • inclusiveness
  • transparency
  • adaptability
  • communication

You want to pick a person you don't mind spending time with, has a shared and common goal with the product. Converse about your problem set, developers naturally want to help you find solutions.

Your people should be able to identify with and tell your story, one that is worth telling, and worth caring about. After your first conversation you can usually pick this up.

Warning

Do not hire people just because they are "smart"

Anyone with a small amount of skill can build a shitty prototype, you will need developers that can carry you past that stage.

Finding A Developer

Great software developers don't send resumes, are rarely on the market, and don't normally look at job postings.

Great software developers don't send resumes, are rarely on the market, or even look at job postings.

Because the market is so tight and good people are so hard to find, really good developers get to work wherever they want.

Conversely bad developers tend to always be on the market and play the resume game like the lottery. Be careful when posting you may end up with a pile of resumes of underwhelming candidates which can be a huge time suck.

Recruiting can be a long process, most of your best bets on finding co-workers will be your past jobs and your social circles.

Most developers will have a visceral disdain for recruiters, thats not to say they are bad. There are some good ones that do provide solid results, but like a good developer good recruiters are hard to find. Word of mouth is a good way to know if a recruiter would be worthwhile.

When writing a job post

Avoid jargon and catchphrases.

Don't:

  • Call developers ninjas, or rockstars.
  • Offer equity as subsidy for low pay.

Do

  • Be upfront.
  • State a salary range.
  • State expected responsibilities, and experience.
  • Explain about the company and product, the kind of culture it might be.

Culture fit

Only let the right ones in, avoid bad hires.

  • Meet them for drinks or dinner, don't talk about programming
  • Have others on your team interview them

Make sure they are a cultural fit, if there are any red flags they will only get redder over time and as stress and timelines sink in.

Warning Signs

  • Programming elitism
  • Misogyny
  • Complaining about former co-workers

There is a huge difference between being able to communicate and relate to touch work experiences and bashing ex-coworkers.

Interns

They do require some extra hand-holding, but can provide surprising results in the right environment.

They do take some extra work but you sometimes win the lottery with this one.

Have a good developer, but can't hire another? Get some interns. With some guidance you can accomplish a lot with some enthusiasm and grit. Their work probably won't be that great but they can provide solid results.

Most developers start early software development, by the time these kids are in their second or third year in college they are pretty good programmers.

Try to get interns before they graduate (2 years before graduating). Ours kept graduating, but their friends would come into our org. They can help with:

  • code quality/ reviews
  • Copy and minor site tweaks
  • QA
  • Exploratory research into other technologies

Retention

  • Don't contract
  • Pay well, or at least competitively
  • Vesting equity as a bonus, not as a subsidy
  • Include developers in design and product talks

Help developers be productive

  • Quite time
  • Keep interruptions to a minimum
  • Schedule meetings around the day's most productive times
  • Good management
  • Clear and transparent communication
